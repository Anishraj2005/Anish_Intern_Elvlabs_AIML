# UNSW-NB15 EDA: Detailed Visualization Interpretation

## 1. Class Distribution (Cell 5)

### What the graph shows

The bar chart and pie chart display the distribution of the target variable (`label`):

- **Normal Traffic (0):** ~36,999 records  
- **Attack Traffic (1):** ~45,332 records  

The pie chart shows:

- **Normal = 44.9%**  
- **Attack = 55.1%**

### Interpretation

The dataset is relatively balanced, although attack records slightly outnumber normal records. This is beneficial because:

- Machine learning models will not be heavily biased toward one class.  
- Accuracy can be a meaningful metric.  
- Extreme resampling techniques may not be necessary.

However, since attacks still dominate the dataset, evaluation metrics such as **precision**, **recall**, **F1-score**, and **ROC-AUC** should still be considered.

### Security Insight

Unlike many cybersecurity datasets where attacks are rare, UNSW-NB15 intentionally includes a large number of attack samples, allowing models to learn malicious patterns effectively.

## 2. Attack Category Distribution (Cell 6)

### What the graph shows

The bar chart displays attack frequencies for each attack category.

Approximate ordering:

| Attack Type    | Count    |
| -------------- | -------- |
| Normal         | ~37,000  |
| Generic        | ~19,000  |
| Exploits       | ~11,000  |
| Fuzzers        | ~6,000   |
| DoS            | ~4,000   |
| Reconnaissance | ~3,500   |
| Analysis       | ~600     |
| Backdoor       | ~500     |
| Shellcode      | ~300     |
| Worms          | Very few |

### Interpretation

The attack classes are highly imbalanced.

#### Dominant Attacks

- **Generic Attacks** – Most frequent category. Includes brute-force activities, cryptographic attacks, and general malicious traffic patterns. These attacks will strongly influence model training.  
- **Exploits** – Second largest. Represents attempts to exploit software vulnerabilities. Important because they often lead to system compromise.  
- **Fuzzers** – Generated by sending unexpected or malformed inputs to discover vulnerabilities.  
- **DoS** – Denial‑of‑Service attacks that attempt to overwhelm resources.  
- **Reconnaissance** – Information gathering (port scanning, host discovery, network mapping), often occurring before larger attacks.

#### Rare Categories

Analysis, Backdoor, Shellcode, and Worms have very few records. This creates a multiclass imbalance problem. A model may struggle to learn these attack types due to insufficient examples, leading to a higher risk of misclassification.

### Security Insight

A classifier may achieve high overall accuracy while performing poorly on Worms, Shellcode, or Backdoor attacks. Therefore, class‑wise evaluation is important.

## 3. Histograms of Numeric Features (Cell 7)

### `dur` (Duration)
- **Observation:** Large spike near zero, long tail toward higher values.  
- **Interpretation:** Most network connections are short‑lived; only a few remain active for long periods. Strongly right‑skewed.

### `sbytes` (Source Bytes)
- **Observation:** Most values concentrated at lower ranges, few extremely large values.  
- **Interpretation:** Most connections transfer small amounts of data; some sessions involve large file transfers or abnormal traffic.

### `dbytes` (Destination Bytes)
- Similar to `sbytes`, highly skewed.

### `rate`
- **Observation:** Extremely concentrated at lower values, long right tail.  
- **Interpretation:** Most connections have low packet rates. Very high rates are often associated with DoS attacks, scanning activities, or automated malicious behavior.

### `sttl` and `dttl`
- **Observation:** Discrete peaks rather than smooth curves.  
- **Interpretation:** TTL values are network protocol parameters. Common operating systems use fixed TTL values (e.g., 64, 128, 255), producing distinct clusters after scaling.

### `sload` and `dload`
- **Observation:** Heavy right skew; many low‑load connections, few extremely high‑load connections.  
- **Interpretation:** Most sessions generate limited throughput; unusually high loads may indicate anomalies.

### `sinpkt` and `dinpkt`
- **Observation:** Strong concentration near lower values with long tails.  
- **Interpretation:** Most packet intervals are short; large gaps could indicate idle sessions, delayed communications, or attack behavior.

### `sjit` and `djit`
- **Observation:** Highly skewed distributions.  
- **Interpretation:** Jitter varies substantially. Unstable packet timing can be a potential indicator of network anomalies.

### Overall Histogram Insight

Most numeric features show non‑normal distributions, heavy skewness, and long tails. This justifies **log transformation**, **standardization**, or **Winsorization** before model training.

## 4. Boxplots After Winsorizing (Cell 8)

### What boxplots show

Boxplots summarize median, quartiles, spread, and remaining outliers.

- **`dur`:** Large spread, median near lower values, still right‑skewed.  
- **`sbytes` / `dbytes`:** Large interquartile ranges, many observations near lower values; Winsorization reduced extreme values.  
- **`rate`:** Still contains some extreme observations (one visible outlier), reflecting traffic rate variability.  
- **`sttl` / `dttl`:** Much narrower spread, discrete behavior remains visible (TTL clustering).

### Interpretation

Winsorization successfully capped extreme values while preserving overall feature distributions. Benefits include reduced impact of outliers, improved model stability, and prevention of domination by extreme observations.

## 5. Correlation Matrix (Cell 9)

This is one of the most important plots. Correlation values range from +1 (perfect positive) to -1 (perfect negative).

### Strong Positive Correlations

| Feature Pair      | Correlation |
| ----------------- | ----------- |
| `rate` ↔ `sload`  | 0.93        |
| `sinpkt` ↔ `sjit` | 0.87        |
| `dinpkt` ↔ `sjit` | 0.85        |
| `sbytes` ↔ `dbytes` | 0.70      |
| `dur` ↔ `sinpkt`  | 0.80        |

- Higher packet rates naturally create higher source load.  
- Connections with larger packet intervals tend to show higher jitter.  
- Bidirectional byte counts are correlated (expected for two‑way communication).

### Strong Negative Correlations

| Feature Pair      | Correlation |
| ----------------- | ----------- |
| `sttl` ↔ `dload`  | -0.82       |
| `sttl` ↔ `dbytes` | -0.70       |

- Traffic with certain TTL characteristics tends to exhibit different destination loads/byte counts – interesting for distinguishing attack traffic.

### Correlation with Label

| Feature | Correlation |
| ------- | ----------- |
| `sttl`  | 0.50        |
| `rate`  | 0.45        |
| `sload` | 0.41        |
| `dload` | -0.45       |
| `sbytes`| -0.33       |
| `dbytes`| -0.31       |

**Interpretation:** The strongest indicators of attack behavior are `sttl`, `rate`, `sload`, and `dload`. These features likely contain valuable information for classification.

### Machine Learning Insight

No feature has correlation close to ±1 with the target. This means:
- No single feature can perfectly identify attacks.  
- Classification requires combining multiple variables.  
- Non‑linear models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, Neural Networks) may perform better.

## 6. Pairplot (Cell 10)

The pairplot compares `rate`, `sttl`, `sbytes`, `dbytes` for **Normal (blue)** vs **Attack (orange)**.

### Diagonal Histograms

- **`rate`:** Attack traffic appears more spread out – attackers generate varying traffic rates.  
- **`sttl`:** Distinct clustering; attack traffic occupies different TTL ranges – strong evidence that TTL helps classification.  
- **`sbytes` / `dbytes`:** Substantial overlap; byte counts alone are not sufficient for separating classes.

### Scatterplots

- **`rate` vs `sttl`:** Some separation; attack samples occupy different regions.  
- **`sbytes` vs `dbytes`:** Heavy overlap – byte counts alone cannot classify attacks.  
- **`sttl` vs `dbytes`:** Clearer clustering, supporting correlation findings.

### Interpretation

Partial separation exists between normal and attack traffic, but no simple linear boundary. This supports the use of tree‑based models, ensemble methods, or deep learning rather than simple linear classifiers.

## 7. Scatterplots for Anomaly Detection (Cell 11)

### `sload` vs `dload` (Log Scale)

**Why log scale?** Without logarithms, large values dominate and small values become invisible. Log scaling reveals patterns across multiple orders of magnitude.

- **Observation:** Most traffic clusters at low loads. Some sparse regions contain very high loads. Attack samples appear more frequently in extreme regions.  
- **Interpretation:** High‑load connections are potential anomalies. Attack traffic often generates high bandwidth usage, floods, or excessive communication rates. This feature pair helps detect DoS attacks, scanning behavior, and automated attacks.

### `sttl` vs `dttl`

- **Observation:** Distinct clusters around specific TTL combinations because TTL values are discrete.  
- **Interpretation:** Different operating systems and attack tools often generate characteristic TTL patterns. Attack traffic occupies some clusters more frequently than normal traffic. Therefore, `sttl` and `dttl` are valuable classification features.

# Overall EDA Conclusions

1. **Dataset Quality:** No major missing‑value issues; suitable for machine learning.  
2. **Class Distribution:** Near‑balanced binary classification – no severe imbalance problem.  
3. **Attack Categories:** Generic and Exploit attacks dominate; Worms and Shellcode are underrepresented.  
4. **Feature Distributions:** Most numeric features are heavily right‑skewed; outliers are common.  
5. **Important Features:** Strong indicators of attack traffic – `sttl`, `rate`, `sload`, `dload`.  
6. **Feature Relationships:** Strong correlations exist among traffic volume features, timing features, and load features.  
7. **Modeling Implication:** Attack detection depends on complex interactions among features rather than a single variable. Models likely to perform well: **Random Forest**, **XGBoost**, **LightGBM**, **Neural Networks** – they can capture the non‑linear relationships visible throughout the EDA.
